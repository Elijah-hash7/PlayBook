01 /
GitHub — Your Real Resume

Before anything else — your GitHub is the first thing engineers and CTOs check. Applications without a solid GitHub get ignored. This is your proof of work. Get it right first.

What your profile needs
  • 01
    Profile README that tells a story
    Not just a skill list. Open with who you are, what you build, and proof — "X deployed projects" hits harder than "I'm learning Node.js". Include tech stack with badges, featured projects, and what you're building next. First 3 lines are what people actually read.
  • 02
    6 pinned repos — best work only
    Pin in order of impressiveness. Backend projects first, then full-stack, then frontend. Unpin anything that looks unfinished, experimental, or has no README. Your profile repo (same name as username) should never be pinned as a project.
  • 03
    Individual project READMEs that explain decisions
    Every pinned repo needs: what it does in plain English, why you built it, the tech stack, architecture overview, setup instructions, live demo link, and most importantly — "What I learned building this". That last section is what engineers actually remember.
  • 04
    Live deployed demos on every serious project
    A repo with no demo is harder to trust. Deploy on Vercel, Railway, Render, or Fly.io. The link in your README needs to actually work. Broken demo links are worse than no demo.
  • 05
    Consistent green contribution graph
    Doesn't need to be perfect but gaps of weeks with zero activity raise questions. Commit daily even if it's small — fixing a bug, updating docs, refactoring. Consistency signals you're actually building.
  • 06
    Real profile picture, name, and bio
    No default avatars. Your bio should be one line: what you build and your specialty. Include your Twitter handle and location (even just your country). Profiles with photos get more connection requests.
  • 07
    One pure backend depth project
    If most of your projects are full-stack or frontend-heavy, build one that shows backend fundamentals hard: proper auth system, rate limiting, caching layer, database design, error handling, environment config. This is what separates backend devs from full-stack generalists.
✓ Do This
  • Write READMEs for the reader, not for yourself
  • Explain why you made technical decisions
  • Keep repos you're proud of public
  • Add architecture diagrams even if simple
  • Link your GitHub everywhere — LinkedIn, Twitter, job applications
  • Star repos you actually use and find interesting
  • Write meaningful commit messages
✗ Don't Do This
  • Pin tutorial follow-along projects
  • Leave READMEs blank or with just "coming soon"
  • Have all repos private with nothing to show
  • Keep broken demo links in your README
  • Commit everything in one giant push
  • Pin your profile README repo as a project
  • Have a default avatar — it signals inactivity

// The Application Rule

3–5 applications per week. Not per day. Every single message needs 2–3 sentences showing you actually looked at what they're building. Reference their specific stack, a feature they ship, or a problem they're solving. One personalized application beats ten copy-paste ones every time. If you can't write one specific sentence about the company — don't apply yet.

✓ Do This
  • Filter by your actual stack — Node.js, TypeScript, backend
  • Apply to companies with 5–50 people — they care about skill not pedigree
  • Personalize every single message — even 2 sentences
  • Follow up once after 1 week if no response
  • Set up email alerts so you apply within 24–48hrs of posting
  • Apply to roles slightly above your level — aim up
✗ Don't Do This
  • Apply to 20+ roles per day — quality collapses
  • Use the same cover message everywhere
  • Apply to FAANG or big corps before you have leverage
  • Ignore the application message field — fill it always
  • Apply without a working GitHub profile
  • Ghost after getting a response — even to decline

// The Discord Rule

Don't join and go silent. Answer one question per day in your most active server. Post what you're building. Help someone debug. Share what you learned. Companies post jobs in Discord before they hit job boards. People casually say "anyone know a backend dev?" weekly. You need to already be known when that moment comes. Proximity beats cold outreach every time.

✓ Do This
  • Join 2–3 servers max and be genuinely active in them
  • Answer questions in your area of knowledge — even partially
  • Share your projects in the appropriate channels
  • Help debug other people's code when you can
  • Ask specific technical questions, not vague ones
  • Engage with people consistently — same username, same energy
✗ Don't Do This
  • Join 15 servers and lurk in all of them
  • Spam "hire me" or drop your GitHub without context
  • Ask questions you could Google in 30 seconds
  • Only show up when you need something
  • Post the same message in multiple channels
  • Be inactive for weeks then suddenly reappear

// The Contribution Rule

Go to repo → Issues tab → filter good first issue or help wanted. Read the CONTRIBUTING.md before touching any code. Comment on the issue saying you'd like to work on it — maintainers appreciate this. Fix a bug, improve a test, clarify documentation. Goal: 1 merged PR in 30–45 days. Small but real. Even doc improvements count. Three to five real contributions changes your credibility entirely.

✓ Do This
  • Read CONTRIBUTING.md before writing any code
  • Comment on the issue first to avoid duplicate work
  • Start small — a bug fix or doc improvement is fine
  • Write clean, well-tested code with clear commit messages
  • Be patient — reviews can take days or weeks
  • Contribute to repos you actually use
✗ Don't Do This
  • Open a PR without commenting on the issue first
  • Spam small meaningless "fixes" to pad your contribution count
  • Contribute to obscure repos nobody recognizes
  • Give up after one rejection — iterate on feedback
  • Open issues without proposing a solution
  • Copy-paste code without understanding what it does

// The Hackathon Rule

Go in as the backend person. Most teams have 2–3 frontend devs and nobody who can build the API layer properly. That's your value. You don't need to know every blockchain — you need to build the backend that connects everything. Register for the next online ETHGlobal or DoraHacks event now, before you feel ready. Submitting anything is worth more than waiting until you're "prepared."

✓ Do This
  • Register early — some events fill team slots fast
  • Go in as the backend dev — that's your value prop
  • Submit even if the project is incomplete
  • Talk to people — connections matter more than prizes
  • Document what you built after — write a Hashnode post
  • Enter online events first to get comfortable
✗ Don't Do This
  • Wait until you "know enough" — you never will
  • Try to build alone when teams are available
  • Focus only on winning — the network is the prize
  • Build something overcomplicated that you can't finish
  • Disappear after the event — follow up with people you met
  • Skip the demo if your project works — always present

// The Writing Rule

Write like you're explaining to a friend who codes. Not a textbook. Not a corporate blog post. Conversational + technical = shareable. Publish on Hashnode first, then cross-post to Dev.to and submit to Hackernoon and Medium publications. One article, four platforms, maximum reach. Post once every two weeks minimum — consistency over frequency always.

✓ Do This
  • Write about things you've actually built — not theory
  • Include code snippets — they get shared
  • Write a specific title — "How I built X" beats "Introduction to X"
  • Cross-post with canonical URL to protect SEO
  • Share in relevant Discord servers after posting
  • Write for the version of you from 6 months ago
✗ Don't Do This
  • Wait until you're "good enough" to write — you already are
  • Write generic "intro to Node.js" articles — too much competition
  • Obsess over perfection — publish and iterate
  • Post only on one platform — cross-post everything
  • Write once and disappear — consistency builds audience
  • Ignore comments — respond to everyone early on

Don't take every gig. Take gigs that produce portfolio pieces. A REST API project you can show is worth more than a quick styling fix you can't. Every contract should add something to your GitHub, your resume, or your network. Small contracts that lead to references are worth more than large anonymous ones.

✓ Do This
  • Create specific service listings — not vague "I build backends"
  • Ask every satisfied client for a review — reviews compound
  • Take backend API gigs that produce showable work
  • Under-promise and over-deliver on first contracts
  • Build on Contra first — zero fees means more money to reinvest
  • Set clear scope before starting — scope creep kills profit
✗ Don't Do This
  • Race to the bottom on price — charge what your time is worth
  • Take gigs outside your actual skill set to seem impressive
  • Work without a contract or scope agreement
  • Ignore clients who give you bad reviews — respond professionally
  • Let freelance distract from building toward full-time
  • Apply to Toptal before you're ready — failed screening can block you

The Right Way to Message Engineers
// Engineer-to-Engineer Message Template

"Hey [Name], I'm a backend dev focused on Node.js and TypeScript. I saw you're building [specific product] — I've actually worked with [relevant technology/API they use] and built [brief relevant thing]. I'd love to know what stack decisions you've found most important at [company size] stage."

Not: "Hi, I'm looking for opportunities at your company..."
Not: "I'd love to connect and learn more about your company..."

You're building a relationship around shared technical interest. The opportunity comes from that, not from asking for it directly.

✓ Do This
  • Message engineers with a specific technical hook — not "I'm looking for work"
  • Reference something specific about their stack or product
  • Be active in communities before you need anything
  • Help people genuinely — referrals come from trust
  • Follow up once after initial message — not more
  • Join Nigerian/African communities — warm networks beat cold outreach
✗ Don't Do This
  • Lead with "I'm looking for a job" in first message
  • Send the same templated message to 50 people
  • Connect and immediately pitch — build first
  • Only network when you desperately need something
  • Ignore people at the same level — they become senior eventually
  • Think LinkedIn premium is required — it's not

Mindset — What Actually Separates Who Makes It

Skills get you in the room. Mindset keeps you in the game long enough for the room to open. Most people quit right before things compound.

🏗️
Build in Public — Not Perfectly
You don't need daily threads. Post what you built, what you learned, what broke. Imperfect and shipped beats perfect and silent. People hire devs they've seen building, not devs they've never heard of.
📊
Depth Beats Breadth
Being a solid backend dev with Web3 exposure beats being okay at everything. Nobody hires "I can do everything." They hire "he's solid at backend AND he's touched Web3." Own your lane first.
⏳
The Timeline Is 3–6 Months
Not 3–6 weeks. Consistent signal for 3–6 months is when interviews start coming. Most people quit at month 2. The ones who don't are the ones who get the roles. Expect the slow build and plan for it.
🔁
Consistency Over Intensity
One article per week beats 10 articles in one week then silence. Three job applications with personalized messages beat 30 copy-paste ones. Small consistent actions compound. Bursts don't.
🎯
Target the Right Companies
Early-stage startups at 5–50 people care about skill, not pedigree or university names. That's where you have the advantage. Stop applying to companies where 500 people applied before you. Apply where your work speaks directly to the founder.
✍️
Writing Is Not Optional
Backend work is invisible. Writing makes it visible. One article that ranks on Google for a specific problem brings more inbound than 50 cold applications. You already have the topics — you've just been keeping the knowledge to yourself.
🌐
Your Location Is Not Your Limit
Remote work has flattened geography. Nigerian devs are working at US, UK, and European startups right now — not because they got lucky but because their GitHub and visibility convinced someone before they even got on a call. That's the play.
💬
Proximity Beats Cold Outreach
Being active in the right Discord server for 30 days beats sending 100 cold LinkedIn messages. When someone says "anyone know a backend dev?" you need to already be in the room. Warm networks win.
